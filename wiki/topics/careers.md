# Careers

Hub for non-research content about working in ML / AI: hiring and interview processes, job-search strategy, compensation structures, negotiation, team dynamics, the implicit norms of the field. Distinct from [[evaluation]] (which is about measuring models) and [[benchmarks]] (which are eval tasks) — this is about the *labor market and career mechanics* around the work.

## Current understanding

One data point so far ([[../sources/2026-sapora-ml-interviews]]), and the most interesting things to note are the *implicit structural claims* of the post rather than its tactical advice:

- **The "top labs" hiring bar is publicly legible.** The author frames the entry condition for callbacks at top labs as "3+ first-author papers (ICLR/NeurIPS/ICML) and at least one industry internship." This is an industry-wide signal being stated openly, not the kind of thing companies put on their job description. Useful as a calibration point for what the funnel actually filters on.
- **Interview prep is converging on a small, reproducible canon.** The technical-topics list (RL / LLMs / Generative modelling / Applied ML / General ML / Linear algebra) reads as the implicit "what an RS hire is expected to know in mid-2026" curriculum. Worth treating as a snapshot — useful for tracking how the canon shifts year over year.
- **LLM mock interviews are now part of the standard prep loop.** Reported as substantively effective (*"surprisingly frequent overlap between those practice questions and what interviewers actually asked"*), not as a gimmick. The asymmetry — using LLMs to prep for interviews that test LLM knowledge — is unremarked-on in the post but obvious from a meta view.
- **Compensation literacy is gated by the reader's geography and structure-awareness.** The RSU vs. stock-options section assumes the reader doesn't already know that startup equity should be discounted heavily and that UK options trigger income tax on exercise. This is a recurring "things that should be common knowledge but aren't" gap in ML career writing.
- **Negotiation leverage tracks peer-recognition, not numerical offer size.** The author flags that competing offers from peer labs (OpenAI, top labs) move numbers; random unknown-startup offers don't. Recruiters reportedly track historical conversion data per competing-offer source. This is a market-signaling result that should generalize.

## Open questions

- How much of the "top labs" prep canon is signal vs. ritual? Which of the technical-topics-list items actually predict on-the-job performance vs. just being part of the hiring shibboleth?
- How does this guide age? The 2026 canon is a snapshot — what's on the list in 2027 / 2028 that isn't here, and what drops off?
- How do hiring norms differ between the "research scientist" track (PhD + papers + RS interviews) and the "research engineer" / "MLE" tracks (no PhD necessary, more system-design weight)? Sapora's post is RS-specific and explicitly avoids generalizing.
- What does the equivalent guide look like from the *hiring manager* side? Recruiter-side incentives and signal-extraction strategies would triangulate the candidate-side advice well.
- Are LLM mock interviews actually a step-change in candidate quality (signal inflation) or just a redistribution of effort? Worth tracking.
